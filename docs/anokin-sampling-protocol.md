### Sampling plan for a close-kin mark recapture study at Lake Kanyaboli, Kenya

This protocol describes a sampling regime for a close-kin mark-recapture study to be conducted at Lake Kanyaboli, western Kenya, which will use genomic relationships between collected mosquitoes to estimate dispersal patterns in *Anopheles funestus*.

**Study design and site selection**

We will perform the study at Lake Kanyaboli, Siaya County, western Kenya. A cross-sectional entomological survey using indoor household aspiration will be carried out across a \~3km long and \~3km wide wedge-shaped sampling area extending from the northeastern corner of Lake Kanyaboli (Figure 1). Sampling will occur over 6 days, with sampling locations arranged in four alternating bands within the wedge. An extra 6 days are available if sample collections are lower than expected. The northeastern corner of the lake has been selected to overlap with a highly productive cluster in the recent LSTM-KEMRI-CDC ATSB trial (cluster 38).

**Household selection**

For each given team on a given morning, we will identify a GPS coordinate within the team’s quadrant. The team will be tasked with locating the four nearest compounds per coordinate and performing sampling in the primary sleeping structure of each compound. If possible, minor structures within the compound should be ignored. Where consent is not given, that house can be ignored, and another house chosen instead.

Two fieldworkers will visit each house, assisted by a community worker/volunteer facilitator. The study aims will be detailed to participants, and consent forms will be explained. Time will be allocated to answer any participant questions, and if participants agree to take part they will be asked to sign the consent form. If consent is provided, the house will be geolocated using GPS within Open-data-kit.

**Entomological sampling method**  
Prokopack automated aspirator (Vazquez-Prokopec et al., 2009\) collections will be performed between 06:00 \- 10:00, at \~25 minutes per house. Sampling teams will work in pairs \+ a community health volunteer, sampling indoors for 15 minutes. Each team will have a single prokopack aspirator (currently being shipped to Kenya). Indoor aspiration collections will start at the main entrance of the house and finish in the rooms at the back of the house to minimise exit of mosquitoes. Collected mosquitoes will be stored in plastic cups, labelled with the household ID and date.

**Storage and identification**  
Collected mosquitoes will be transported to either the field laboratory at Lwak as soon as the morning collections are completed, where all adult mosquitoes will be counted and morphologically identified to genus. Sex will be recorded, and specimens will be stored in Eppendorf tubes with silica gel for genomic analysis.

**Data management and analysis**  
Entomological data will be collected on handheld tablet devices, using the following Open Data Kit (ODK) form with drop down boxes and consistency checks to avoid errors in data entry and collection. 

* Collector Name  
* Consent  
* Date collected  
* House identifier (VillageID-houseID-date)  
* GPS coordinates  
* Wall type  
* Roof type  
* Start Time  
* End Time

Following sampling each morning, Sanjay will quality control the data synced with ODK and feedback to teams.
