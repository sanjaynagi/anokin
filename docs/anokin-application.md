Application Information B.1. Project Title (max 50 words)  AnoKin: Using genomics to estimate dispersal in malaria mosquitoes B.2. Project Summary (max 100 words) Describe the research in simple terms in a way that could be publicised to a general audience. If successful, this summary will be made available on LSTM website. Malaria remains the deadliest infectious disease in humans, and in recent years, progress towards elimination has stalled. Elimination will require developing new vector control tools and a complete understanding of mosquito population dynamics, yet we still do not understand how far malaria mosquitoes can disperse. This basic knowledge gap impedes research advances in our field; it obstructs the design of cluster-RCTs for new vector control tools, obscures estimates of gene drive spread, and limits our understanding of the spread of insecticide resistance. This project will utilise genomics to quantify dispersal in the major malaria mosquito, *Anopheles funestus*. We will apply a novel technique, close-kin mark-recapture, in which we sequence the genomes of individuals, and then estimate kin to infer dispersal rates and distances. This will provide critical data to enhance and optimize vector control efforts.  B.3. Rationale  Malaria is a vector-borne disease transmitted by *Anopheles* mosquitoes, causing substantial mortality and morbidity, particularly in sub-Saharan Africa. The most successful malaria control methods to date have been based on suppressing the insect vector [(Bhatt *et al.*, 2015\)](https://paperpile.com/c/SXa6VI/v3Ia9). These are reliant upon insecticides, for example, in long-lasting insecticidal nets (LLINs) or indoor residual spraying (IRS). Unfortunately, insecticide resistance threatens the progress of malaria control programmes [(Hemingway *et al.*, 2016\)](https://paperpile.com/c/SXa6VI/tOkuG). Even before the recent stalling in progress against malaria, it has been long recognised that novel vector control tools will be necessary to reduce the burden further [(Killeen *et al.*, 2023\)](https://paperpile.com/c/SXa6VI/G7hjd). We therefore must be able to evaluate new products (such as spatial repellents and ATSBs) effectively in robust randomised controlled trials (RCTs). A particularly promising tool is gene drive, where transgenes are introduced into the population to either spread and cause suppression [(Kyrou *et al.*, 2018\)](https://paperpile.com/c/SXa6VI/WiKEl) or by introducing a gene which makes the vector population refractory to malaria transmission [(Gantz *et al.*, 2015\)](https://paperpile.com/c/SXa6VI/AWERe). The large gene drive consortiums, such as Target Malaria and Zero Transmission, under pressure from funders to make gene drive a reality, are aiming to design field trials of their respective gene drive systems by 2028\. To accurately model the spread of gene drives and design vector control trials effectively, we must know how far mosquitoes can disperse. Currently, this is a somewhat unknown quantity; traditional estimates suggest mosquitoes fly no more than 5km per generation [(Costantini *et al.*, 1996; Service, 1997\)](https://paperpile.com/c/SXa6VI/BQQ92+5Atdp), however, recent studies in the Sahel and western Kenya have also suggested that they may be carried by wind currents, enabling travel over extremely large distances [(Huestis *et al.*, 2019; Atieli *et al.*, 2023\)](https://paperpile.com/c/SXa6VI/8RbXu+0QFMJ).  Genomics has revolutionised vector surveillance, but so far most advances have been related to mechanisms and the geographic extent of insecticide resistance [(Clarkson *et al.*, 2018\)](https://paperpile.com/c/SXa6VI/cI89V). In AnoKin, I aim to use genomics in a translational context, to estimate the distances over which malaria vectors can disperse. We will utilise an advanced genomic technique known as close-kin mark-recapture [(Bravington *et al.*, 2016; Schmidt *et al.*, 2023\)](https://paperpile.com/c/SXa6VI/XPoYQ+r6Gvn). In this approach, individuals are sampled and their genomes sequenced. Based on the resulting data, familial relationships are established between pairs. Upon inference of close-kin, it is then possible to use the geographic distances between sampled kin to estimate mean dispersal distances [(Filipović *et al.*, 2020\)](https://paperpile.com/c/SXa6VI/e2tKK). Close-kin mark-recapture was originally pioneered for use in fisheries, and as well as dispersal can be used to estimate census population size and daily survivorship. Aims Implement a spatially explicit sampling framework at Lake Kanyaboli, western Kenya.  In collaboration with Dr. Eric Ochomo (LSTM and KEMRI-Kisumu), we will implement a spatially explicit sampling framework [(Sedda *et al.*, 2019\)](https://paperpile.com/c/SXa6VI/wZyXI) at Lake Kanyaboli, western Kenya. Lake Kanyaboli is an area of high malaria transmission with the lake providing a stable habitat for large numbers of *Anopheles funestus* [(Omondi *et al.*, 2023\)](https://paperpile.com/c/SXa6VI/PLFe6). Dr. Ochomo has installed experimental huts at the site and established it as an ideal location for malaria vector research [(Ogutu *et al.*, 2024; Agumba *et al.*, 2024\)](https://paperpile.com/c/SXa6VI/cXpmn+5Kygn). Maps of Lake Kanyaboli are shown in Figure 2\. We will establish sample collection sites that are stratified for different ecological zones in the area, ensuring that mosquito collections are not biased with a proportional lattice with close-pairs design [(Sedda et al. 2019](https://paperpile.com/c/SXa6VI/wZyXI)). At each site, we will deploy UV light traps indoors in houses and collect adult *An. funestus* mosquitoes over a period of three weeks. All collected mosquitoes will be individually stored on silica and metadata recorded (such as precise GPS coordinates). We will then send our sampled mosquitoes to a preferred commercial sequencing partner for whole-genome sequencing. We aim to sequence a total of 600 individuals at a coverage of 15X.  In previous studies, we have found preliminary evidence of kinship both within and between villages. Along with the methodological advancement in Objective 2, this sampling framework will enable us to describe the full distribution of dispersal events.  Infer dispersal distances   We will submit our whole-genome sequence data to our partner MalariaGEN for initial bioinformatic analysis, such as extensive quality control, variant calling, phasing, and taxonomic analysis. This will also enable accession and population genomic analysis of the data from the cloud, via the open source Python package malariagen\_data ([https://malariagen.github.io/malariagen-data-python/latest/index.html](https://malariagen.github.io/malariagen-data-python/latest/index.html)), increasing accessibility of the data and transparency of our project. I will apply relatedness estimation software (NGSRelate) to infer pairwise kinship coefficients between all pairs of individuals \- we have shown in prior research that this is effective in identifying close-kin up to 2nd-degree relations (aunt/uncle, grandparent/grandchild, niece/nephew) [(Lucas et al. 2023\)](https://paperpile.com/c/SXa6VI/gtru). In parallel, in collaboration with Dr. Alistair Miles (Wellcome Sanger Institute), I am developing a method to infer segments of identity-by-descent between pairs of mosquitoes, which will allow me to more accurately estimate kinship at lower levels of relatedness of up to 3rd-degree relatives. Segments of Identity-by-descent (IBD) are stretches of DNA that are inherited from a common ancestor without any intervening recombination. This permits a large increase in study power, as 3rd-degree kin are expected to be frequent in our dataset. I will add the code to estimate IBD tracts and kinship into malariagen\_data, enabling other researchers in the field to perform the same analyses.  For each pair of kin identified, we will calculate the geographic distance between their sampling locations. We will then use these distances to build a dispersal kernel and to estimate the mean and maximum per-generation dispersal distances for *An. funestus* in our study area. We will also investigate potential sex-biased dispersal patterns by comparing distances between male-male, female-female, and male-female kin pairs, and  identify ecological and landscape factors that influence dispersal . Although AnoKin is focused on dispersal, CKMR data can also reveal other demographic parameters such as census population size and survivorship, which could be applied in the context of vector control trials to estimate intervention effects. This will be explored after the primary aim of dispersal quantification is achieved. Finally, we will build a public webpage to explain the project itself and to show and communicate the resulting analyses. Along with a high-impact publication we expect that this will increase the likelihood of follow-on funding, as well as highlighting the work that we do at LSTM and the DCF itself. For an example of how this may look, here is a previous example of a web resource I and Dr. Miles built for a recent project, the *malaria vector selection atlas* ([https://tinyurl.com/sel-atlas](https://tinyurl.com/sel-atlas)).
